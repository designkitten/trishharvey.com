Washington, D.C. Metropolitan Area

The Washington, D.C. metropolitan area is a 4,000 square-mile region consisting of the District of Columbia; the Virginia counties of Loudoun, Fairfax, Arlington, Prince William, and Stafford; the Virginia cities of Fairfax, Falls Church, Manassas, Manassas Park, and Alexandria; and the Maryland counties of Calvert, Charles, Frederick, Montgomery, and Prince George’s. The home of our nation’s capital and the country’s most highly educated work-force, Washington D.C. is also a strategic East Coast location linking national and international markets via an excellent transportation network, offering a balance of private sector and government organizations. The area is one of the most consistent regions in the country in terms of economic strength, stability, and continuous growth.

The Washington, D.C. metropolitan area is the fifth largest metropolitan statistical area in the United States, with a population of over four million. According to the Bureau of Labor and Statistics, the Washington, D.C. SMSA is the country’s fourth largest employment market with over two million jobs. Historically dependent on the Federal Government, the Washington, D.C. metropolitan area economy has become broadly diversified in the past 25 years. Today, the Federal Government accounts for less than 17% of the area’s employment. Although not the largest employer, the Federal Government does help to stabilize the area’s’s economy, with direct employment and the procurement of goods and services for the local area. Despite defense and other federal employment cutbacks, federal procurement in the Washington, D.C. area have grown steadily over the past five years resulting in substantial private sector employment growth. As can be illustrated by the fact that of every federal procurement dollar that is spent nationally, over 50 cents is spent in the Washington, D.C. metropolitan area.

The public sector in the Washington, D.C. area is more than balanced by a substantial and diverse private sector. This diversity is illustrated by the types of major corporations headquartered in the area, including Mobil Oil, Marriott International/Host Marriott, General Dynamics, MCI, Gannett, Mars, America Online, US Airways, and Lockheed/Martin, as well as significant financial institutions such as the World Bank, the International Monetary Fund, Inter-American Development Bank, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and Sallie Mae. In fact, virtually all of the Fortune 500 companies have a presence in the area. In addition, a substantial percentage of the private sector is comprised of industry segments that have excellent potential for future growth. These include telecommunications, computer software and systems integration, internet services, biotechnology, health related services, finance, industry associations, and professional services.

The metropolitan area has the best educated work force in the nation. When compared to other major markets, Washington ranks first in the nation, with the highest percentage of professional and technical employment categories, highest income levels, and the highest percentage of women in the work force.

This healthy economic diversification and strong job growth has resulted in well over 250 million square feet of office space that has doubled in size in the past fifteen years and continues to grow. The metropolitan area possesses one of the lowest office vacancy rates in the country.  In the recent real estate downturn, the issue for many area was negative demand; the problem in Washington was oversupply. Throughout the 1990-1992 recession the Washington office market never experienced negative absorption, which allowed for a quicker recovery.

The Washington, D.C. area also benefits from one of the best overall transportation systems in the country. The Washington MetroRail System is among the most efficient and convenient in the world, providing excellent service to major parts of Suburban Maryland, Northern Virginia and the District of Columbia offering a viable alternative to roadway travel. The construction of the MetroRail has spurred new development in downtown Washington and its suburbs with major new office and mixed-use projects being built around the individual Metro stops that have had a major impact on land values around each station. In particular, former lesser desirable portions of downtown Washington and Arlington County have experienced economic revitalization due to the opening of MetroRail.

The Washington area is served by three major airports: Washington National, Washington Dulles International, and Baltimore/Washington International(BWI). Together these airports make the area the fourth largest travel market in the nation. The primary road network is comprised of Interstates 495, 395, 295, 95, 270, and 66; U.S. Routes 50, 29, and 1; Virginia Routes 267 (Dulles Toll Road), 123, and 7; and, the George Washington and Baltimore/Washington Parkways.
